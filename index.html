Quick context, if this industry is new to you

Insurance is a deal: you (or your family) pay a company a bit of money regularly — monthly or yearly — and in return, if something bad happens to your car, home, or health, the company helps pay to put it right. That company is called the insurer. You might also hear the word broker in this industry — that's someone who helps a customer find and manage the right insurance, rather than insuring things themselves.

An insurance claim is what happens after something goes wrong — a car gets dinged, a pipe bursts, a phone screen cracks — and the customer asks for that promised help. Behind the scenes, the claim has to be logged, checked, priced, and paid out, often while the customer is stressed and just wants it sorted quickly.

CLM-1842 is a tiny but real example of the kind of improvement that makes that moment less painful: instead of phoning up to describe the damage, the customer just takes a photo. Multiply this by thousands of small features like it, and that's most of what a software team in this industry actually spends its time building.

A real AI use case

What if CLM-1842 used AI too?

Here's a realistic next step for the same claims-photo feature you've followed through this whole site — and exactly where each of the four new roles above would actually touch it.

Proposed feature

Automatically estimate the type and severity of damage from the photo a customer uploads, so the claims handler sees a head start instead of a blank photo to assess from scratch.

AI/ML Engineer Trains the model on thousands of past labelled claim photos so it can recognise patterns like dents, cracks, and scrapes.
Prompt Engineer / AI Interaction Designer Shapes how the model explains its reasoning in plain English, so the handler can see why it reached that assessment.
AI Product Manager Decides how confident the model needs to be before its assessment is trusted without a human double-check.
MLOps / AI Platform Engineer Watches the model's accuracy over time — claim photos in winter, at night, or after a storm can all look different to what it was trained on.
The honest caveat: the model can get it wrong, especially on unusual or rare types of damage. That's exactly why a claims handler still reviews every assessment rather than acting on it automatically — the AI speeds things up, it doesn't replace the judgement call.
